This setup doesn‚Äôt tell us the exact voltage, but it gives us something better: a visual indicator under load. If the LED lights up, the battery has enough voltage and current to be worth using. So since this LED isn't lit, does that mean that the battery's bad?

Well, let‚Äôs do the math (Ohm‚Äôs Law style). Ohm‚Äôs Law is: V = I x R, where V is voltage, I is current, and R is resistance. Pretty basic. We can tell whether a battery has any power left by checking the current (I). If we solve this equation for current, it's I = (V_battery - V_forward) / R.

You'll noticed my solved form of Ohm's law has a more complex voltage "mini-equation": (V_battery - V_forward). V_forward represents the Forward voltage of the LED, which is sometimes also called the threshold voltage. That's the voltage it takes get get current to even flow through the LED.

These forward voltages are different for different colors of LEDs. We can use that our advantage if we do it right -- but we'll get to that later. In the meantime, have a look at this chart:

Let's try some combinations

You need to know the characteristics of your components and then ensure sufficient voltage to drive them (note that I still have a 330-ohm resistor in the circuit -- we'll get to that in a minute). It's clear from the chart above that 1.5 VDC won't drive (turn on) a green LED. But what if we try a red one, which has a lower forward voltage?

A red LED lights up some with 1.5 VDC and a 330-ohm resistor.

Well! We get some light from that red LED. How come that happens? I mean, the chart above says that just won't work. But it does. In fact, this particular make of red LED will light up, at least a little, with 1.5 VDC -- apparently. You do the math all day long, but if it's lit, it's lit.

This goes back to what I say on my homepage: You have to read a little, calculate a little, and then try some things. All of those steps together tell you what really works and doesn't.

This brings us to a cardinal rule of electronics: Never trust theory, calculation, or practice alone, but never discount any of those, either. You have to gather the information first (the LED chart), do some rudimentary calculations (as we did above) to get yourself into a relatively safe range for testing, and then you have to test it yourself. Nothing else works when building circuits. Nothing.

Don't look for complicated solutions to simple problems

The picture above is a good example of another best practice you might think about adopting: Don't find complicated solutions to simple problems. You see some teacher's stick-on putty and a couple of piece of bent metal in the drawing, but as you also see, they didn't help the problem. The simplest solution -- using scotch tape to attach the leads to the two batteries, and then just pressing the batteries together -- solved the problem really quickly. And, of course, getting a longer piece of wire to extend the circuit a bit.
